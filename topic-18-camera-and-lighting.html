Camera and lighting are powerful technical codes. The shots you choose, how you frame a subject and the way you light the scene all change how the audience feels and what they notice.

What Do Camera and Lighting Do in a Media Product?

In R093, you must understand how camera work and lighting are used as technical codes. They help producers:

  • Draw attention to important details or characters.
  • Create a specific mood (e.g. tense, exciting, calm).
  • Represent characters in a certain way (powerful, weak, friendly, mysterious).
  • Support the purpose of the product (e.g. persuade, inform, entertain).

Key Camera Concepts

  • Shot types ‚Äì how much of the subject and background is shown.
  • Angles ‚Äì where the camera is positioned (high, low, eye-level).
  • Movement ‚Äì how the camera moves (pan, tilt, tracking, handheld).
  • Framing and composition ‚Äì rule of thirds, headroom and lead room.

Key Lighting Concepts

  • High-key lighting ‚Äì bright, low contrast, minimal shadows.
  • Low-key lighting ‚Äì strong contrast, deep shadows, dramatic mood.
  • Colour gels ‚Äì coloured lights used to change mood (e.g. red, blue, green).
  • Natural vs artificial light ‚Äì daylight compared with studio lights or LED panels.

  • Angles: high angle to make a subject look weak, low angle to make them look powerful.
  • Movement: pans and tilts to follow action, tracking shots to move with characters.
  • Framing: rule of thirds, headroom and lead room to keep compositions balanced.
  • Focus: shallow depth of field to highlight one subject, deep focus to show the whole scene.

Camera Shots, Angles and Movement

Camera choices are deliberate. In the exam, you must be able to identify shot types, angles and movement, then explain their effect on the audience.

Shot Types

  • Extreme long shot (ELS) ‚Äì shows a wide area or landscape.
    Effect: establishes the setting and scale of the scene.
  • Long shot (LS) ‚Äì shows the whole subject, usually head to toe.
    Effect: lets the audience see body language and surroundings.
  • Mid shot (MS) ‚Äì usually shows a person from the waist up.
    Effect: balances facial expression with context, common in interviews.
  • Close-up (CU) ‚Äì shows a face or single object in detail.
    Effect: highlights emotion or focuses on an important prop.
  • Extreme close-up (ECU) ‚Äì focuses on one detail (eyes, a button, a logo).
    Effect: creates intensity and makes the detail feel important or dramatic.

Camera Angles

  • High angle ‚Äì camera looks down on the subject.
    Effect: can make them seem weaker, smaller or less powerful.
  • Low angle ‚Äì camera looks up at the subject.
    Effect: makes them appear powerful, confident or threatening.
  • Eye-level angle ‚Äì camera is level with the subject‚Äôs eyes.
    Effect: feels natural and neutral, like a normal conversation.

Camera Movement

  • Pan ‚Äì camera turns left or right from a fixed position.
    Effect: follows action or reveals more of the scene.
  • Tilt ‚Äì camera moves up or down.
    Effect: can reveal height, make a character look powerful, or show something important.
  • Tracking / dolly shot ‚Äì camera moves along with the subject.
    Effect: adds energy and helps the audience feel part of the action.
  • Handheld camera ‚Äì camera is held by hand, creating a shaky effect.
    Effect: makes footage feel realistic or chaotic, like a documentary or POV.

Lighting and Mood

Lighting helps to set the mood, highlight important parts of the frame and support the message of the product. Different lighting styles create different reactions in the audience.

Lighting Styles

  • High-key lighting ‚Äì bright and evenly lit.
    Used for: comedies, daytime TV, adverts aimed at families or children.
    Effect: creates a positive, safe and friendly atmosphere.
  • Low-key lighting ‚Äì lots of shadows and contrast.
    Used for: thrillers, horror, dramatic scenes.
    Effect: builds tension, mystery or fear.
  • Three-point lighting ‚Äì key, fill and back light.
    Used for: interviews, studio filming, professional looks.
    Effect: separates the subject from the background and looks polished.

Colour and Gels

  • Warm colours (orange, yellow, red)
    Effect: feel cosy, energetic or urgent depending on context.
  • Cool colours (blue, green)
    Effect: suggest calm, technology or sometimes sadness.
  • Coloured gels placed over lights
    Effect: change the emotional tone of a scene and support branding or genre.

Natural vs Artificial Light

  • Natural light ‚Äì sunlight, daylight from windows.
    Effect: can feel realistic, fresh and informal.
  • Artificial light ‚Äì studio lights, LEDs, spotlights.
    Effect: gives more control, can be dramatic and stylised.
